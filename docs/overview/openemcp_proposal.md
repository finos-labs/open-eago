# Open Enterprise Multi-Agent Communication and Orchestration Protocol (OpenEMCP)

In the rapidly evolving landscape of multinational banking, AI-driven agentic systems hold immense potential to streamline operations, enhance decision-making, and improve efficiency in areas like fraud detection, risk assessment, and customer service.

However, the sector is plagued by significant challenges stemming from stringent regulatory oversight, frequent policy updates, and the complexities of cross-border activities. These include cascading compliance risks, data privacy vulnerabilities, and limitations on AI delegation that can lead to fines, operational disruptions, and reduced innovation. For instance, heterogeneous data protection laws across jurisdictions, such as GDPR in the EU and sector-specific rules in the US, create barriers to seamless AI deployment, often resulting in increased costs and legal exposure. Frequent regulatory changes—driven by bodies like the European Banking Authority (EBA), Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), and emerging frameworks like the EU AI Act—exacerbate these issues, requiring agile systems that incorporate planning, agent registries, and built-in compliance mechanisms.

To address this, a new agentic protocol, such as an enhanced OpenEMCP (Open Enterprise Multi-Agent Communication and Orchestration Protocol), could provide a foundational layer for secure, regulatory compliant AI interactions. Unlike existing frameworks, this protocol would prioritize privacy-preserving delegation, geo-aware enforcement, and adaptive policy integration, justifying its development by mitigating risks like third-party dependencies, cyber vulnerabilities, and jurisdictional conflicts that currently hinder AI efficiency in global banking. By building toward a full framework (GenGenni) later, this protocol lays the groundwork for resilient, scalable agentic systems tailored to regulated environments.

## Acronyms and Definitions

**EBA** stands for the European Banking Authority. It is an independent EU regulatory agency responsible for ensuring effective and consistent prudential regulation and supervision across the European banking sector. The EBA develops binding technical standards, guidelines, and recommendations to create a harmonized "Single Rulebook" for banks in the EU, promotes supervisory convergence, conducts stress tests, and assesses risks to financial stability. It plays a key role in areas like outsourcing, operational resilience (e.g., under DORA), and data governance for financial institutions.

**DOJ** refers to the U.S. Department of Justice. In the context of the phrase, you quoted ("fines under EBA and DOJ"), it relates to a 2025 DOJ Final Rule implementing Executive Order 14117. This rule restricts or prohibits certain cross-border transfers of bulk U.S. sensitive personal data (e.g., health, financial, genomic data) and government-related data to "countries of concern" (such as China, Russia, Iran, North Korea, Cuba, and Venezuela) or associated entities. Violations can result in civil or criminal penalties (fines) enforced by the DOJ's National Security Division.

**PSD2** stands for the Payment Services Directive 2 (Directive (EU) 2015/2366). It is an EU directive that regulates payment services and payment service providers throughout the European Union (EU) and European Economic Area (EEA), aiming to enhance competition, innovation, and consumer protection in the payments sector. PSD2 introduced open banking requirements, mandating banks to provide secure APIs for licensed third-party providers (TPPs) to access customer account information and initiate payments with explicit consent, while enforcing Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) to reduce fraud. It plays a key role in areas like data sharing, third-party access, security standards, and operational resilience for financial institutions. As of late 2025, PSD2 remains in force but is being phased toward PSD3 (proposed in 2023 with ongoing negotiations), which addresses gaps in enforcement, fraud prevention, and extension to emerging payment types. Violations can result in supervisory actions, fines, or sanctions enforced by national competent authorities under EBA oversight.

**OCC** stands for the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency. It is a U.S. federal agency that charters, regulates, and supervises national banks, federal savings associations, and federal branches of foreign banks, ensuring safety, soundness, and fair access to financial services. The OCC issues guidelines on risk management, third-party relationships, and emerging technologies like AI, with enforcement powers including supervisory actions and fines for non-compliance.

**GDPR** stands for the General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679). It is an EU law on data protection and privacy for individuals within the European Union and EEA, governing the processing of personal data by organizations worldwide if targeting EU residents. GDPR mandates principles like data minimization, consent, and rights to access/erasure, with strong enforcement by national authorities and the EDPB, including fines up to 4% of global turnover for violations.

**DPA** refers to a Data Processing Agreement (or Data Processing Addendum). It is a legally binding contract required under GDPR Article 28 between data controllers and processors, outlining obligations for secure, compliant personal data processing. DPAs specify roles, security measures, sub-processing rules, and breach notification, ensuring accountability; non-compliance can lead to fines enforced by national data protection authorities.

**DORA** stands for the Digital Operational Resilience Act (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554). It is an EU regulation strengthening ICT risk management and operational resilience for financial entities (banks, insurers, etc.), covering incident reporting, testing, and third-party oversight. DORA harmonizes rules across the EU financial sector, with oversight by ESAs (including EBA), and includes penalties for non-compliance enforced by national authorities.

**BCBS** stands for the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision. It is an international committee hosted by the Bank for International Settlements (BIS), comprising central banks and supervisory authorities to enhance global banking supervision through standards like Basel frameworks. BCBS develops guidelines on capital, risk management, and data aggregation (e.g., BCBS 239), influencing national regulations without direct enforcement powers but via member implementation.

**BCBS 239** refers to Principles for Effective Risk Data Aggregation and Risk Reporting by the Basel Committee. It sets 14 principles for banks to improve data accuracy, timeliness, and reporting during crises, enhancing governance and IT infrastructure. Implementation is monitored nationally (e.g., by EBA or OCC), with supervisory actions for non-compliance rather than direct fines.

**Basel III** is a global regulatory framework developed by the BCBS post-2008 crisis to strengthen bank capital requirements, liquidity, and leverage ratios. It enhances risk coverage, introduces buffers for systemic stability, and promotes resilience against financial shocks. Implemented nationally (e.g., via EU CRR/CRD or U.S. rules), with supervisory enforcement and potential penalties for non-compliance by regulators like EBA or OCC.

**EU AI Act** stands for the Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689). It is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, classifying AI systems by risk (prohibited, high-risk, limited, minimal) and imposing obligations like conformity assessments for high-risk uses in finance (e.g., credit scoring). Enforced by national authorities and the EU AI Office, with fines up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover for violations.

**CCPA** stands for the California Consumer Privacy Act (as amended by CPRA). It is a U.S. state law granting California residents rights over personal data collection/sale by businesses, including opt-out, access, deletion, and non-discrimination. CCPA applies to large companies processing Californians' data, enforced by the California Attorney General and private actions, with fines up to $7,500 per intentional violation.

**FATF AML** refers to the Financial Action Task Force's Anti-Money Laundering standards. The FATF is an intergovernmental body setting global recommendations (40 Recommendations) to combat money laundering, terrorist financing, and proliferation. It promotes risk-based approaches, customer due diligence, and reporting, with mutual evaluations; non-compliance can lead to gray/blacklisting and enforcement by national authorities.

**EDPB** stands for the European Data Protection Board. It is an independent EU body comprising national data protection authorities, ensuring consistent GDPR application through guidelines, opinions, and one-stop-shop dispute resolution. The EDPB promotes cooperation and binding decisions in cross-border cases, supporting enforcement without direct fining powers but influencing national penalties.

**SR 11-7** refers to Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management issued by the U.S. Federal Reserve and OCC. It provides principles for effective model development, validation, and governance in banks, covering risks from AI/ML models in credit, compliance, etc. Enforcement is supervisory via exams and corrective actions, without statutory fines but potential for broader penalties.

**RBAC** stands for Role-Based Access Control. It is a security policy model assigning permissions to users based on predefined roles rather than individuals, ensuring least-privilege access in systems like banking IAM. RBAC supports compliance with regulations (e.g., GDPR, SOX) by simplifying audits and segregation of duties; poor implementation can lead to regulatory findings and enforcement actions.

**arXiv** (pronounced "archive") is a free, open-access online repository and preprint server primarily for scholarly articles in physics, mathematics, computer science, quantitative biology, quantitative finance, statistics, electrical engineering, systems science, and economics. Founded in 1991 by physicist Paul Ginsparg at Los Alamos National Laboratory and now operated by Cornell University, it enables researchers to rapidly share preprints (before peer review) and post prints to accelerate scientific dissemination and collaboration. Submissions are moderated for relevance and quality (with an endorsement system for new users) but not peer-reviewed, and as of late 2025, arXiv hosts nearly 2.4 million articles, supported by Cornell, the Simons Foundation, and institutional contributions, playing a pivotal role in open science by timestamping discoveries and bypassing traditional journal delays or paywalls.
